6 Key Takeaways from the Convening

Here are the through-lines that ran across every table, every session, every post-it.

Equity and Youth Voice at the Core

While equity has been implicit in MCert's design, it must now be explicitly named and operationalized. Lived experience, specifically for BIPOC, LGBTQ+, and disabled youth, must be baked into the roots of the standards, not treated as a later addition. Intersectionality shapes how we define quality, who we center, and what success looks like.

Organizing + Channeling Demand, Through Centering Evaluation

MCert should position itself as an organizer of existing demand — serving as a cross-ecosystem connector and problem-solving tool rather than just a standalone certification body. MCert wins if it becomes the infrastructure layer for trust and alignment, not just a stamp of approval.

Certification as Movement Signal, Not an End

MCert should function as the visible signal of a broader movement, requiring clear identity, audience, and meaning to drive long-term awareness, trust, and funding. Certification becomes a visible interface of the larger system. It must function as a tool that unlocks outcomes.

Clarity on Audience, Function, and Product Strategy

MCert must prioritize a clear initial audience and role — acting as an intermediary that delivers specific value (trust, compliance, or adoption) through a focused product strategy and defining the role of digital tools in achieving well-being and mental health. Focus and sequencing matter more than completeness.

Defining & Proving "Quality" Across Stakeholders

MCert needs to define and prove "quality" by balancing safety, risk, outcomes, and engagement — grounded in measurable results aligned with how youth actually perceive value. MCert must earn trust through measurable results, not positioning alone. Results also need to be in a concise timeline, no more than a year for payors.

Choosing a Sustainable Role & Business Model

MCert's long-term impact depends on choosing a focused role, building a sustainable model aligned with high-leverage partners such as payers and states, and advancing equity as an NGO. Balance mission (equity) with mechanisms of scale (funding and policy). MCert's long-term viability depends on aligning with actors who have money and enforcement power.

The Day

How the day unfolded.

Six sessions. One arc — from surfacing tensions to shared vision to standards to what comes next.

1
Morning · Connection Activity
Surfacing the Tensions
The room named 18 tensions at the intersection of technology, mental health, and youth well-being — from funding realities to AI literacy gaps to regulatory lag. The honest acknowledgment that none of these are easy set the tone for everything that followed.
AI & Tech Funding Regulation
2
Morning · Problem Pulse Check
Aligning on the Problem
The group grounded MCert's work in who these tools are for and what barriers exist. Key: centering marginalized voices, accounting for peer-first help-seeking, the scarcity of free accessible tools, and the ethical stakes of AI built on marginalized labor.
Youth Voice Accessibility Equity
3
Morning · Shared Vision Activity
Dreaming the Possible
Participants centered a young person in the room and imagined a world where the weight of the mental health crisis has been lifted. The vision: empowered youth with two kinds of literacy, real agency, and safe spaces — online and off — to play and thrive. Youth as solution, not problem.
Vision Empowerment Joy
4
Late Morning · Quality Standards
Testing the Framework
The five-step risk-based evaluation framework was stress-tested. The room pushed on equity gaps, AI checkboxes becoming outdated, the breadth of ages 11–26, measuring unintended outcomes, youth trust in certification seals, and the critical need for future-proof, tech-agnostic standards.
Standards Risk Evidence
5
Afternoon · Mini-SWOT
What Would Make This Work
Four groups identified enabling incentives, feasibility risks, and the one unlock that would move the whole system. The convergence: a simple, trusted, widely recognized signal — like a restaurant grade or Common Sense Media rating — that anyone can act on. Plus sustained awareness and funding to make it a movement, not a moment.
Adoption Business Model Signal
6
Afternoon · Priorities & Next Steps
From Alignment to Action
The final session moved from vision to roadmap. Schools as sites of service, payers and states as sustainable partners, the colander metaphor for MCert's filtering role, and a sharp focus on starting risk-first with one tool, one place, one founder — then expanding from proof.
Strategy Next Steps Funding

What's Next

Four things we're working on now.

Based on your feedback, here is how MCert is moving forward.

01

Define North Star

Define a plain-language statement of what the MCert seal means — a simple explanation of the problem MCert solves, what our role is, and who we serve (both short and long term).

02

Business Model Mapping

Study existing standards and business models and survey different stakeholders on what they'd pay for and where they see value.

03

Pick a Starting Point

We cannot serve everyone at once, and we want to know the quickest way we can make impact in the short-term while looking at our long-term goals.

04

Build Partnerships + Knowledge Base

Identify the education, data, and shared understanding that key stakeholders need right now and find the funding and partnership structures that can deliver it.

MCert needs collaborators, not just supporters.
